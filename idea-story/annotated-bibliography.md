# Annotated Bibliography: Research Sources for "What's the Story Behind...?" GPT

**Project:** Custom GPT for Research-Based Concept Origins Exploration  
**Date:** November 2025  
**Purpose:** Documentation of scholarly and professional sources that informed the development of system instructions and knowledge base

## OVERVIEW

This annotated bibliography documents the research conducted to develop evidence-based methodologies for the "What's the Story Behind...?" GPT. Sources are organized by topic area and include reliability assessments plus specific explanations of how each source informed the final deliverables.

## INTELLECTUAL HISTORY METHODOLOGY

### Wikipedia. (2025). Intellectual history. Retrieved from https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intellectual_history

**Reliability:** Medium-High - Wikipedia entry with extensive citations; used only as starting point to identify key concepts and scholars

**How Used:** Provided overview of Lovejoy's unit-idea methodology (identifying and tracing conceptual building blocks across eras), Cambridge School contextualism (understanding ideas within historical contexts), and Begriffsgeschichte (German conceptual history tracing meaning changes over time). Identified key figures including Quentin Skinner, Reinhart Koselleck, and J.G.A. Pocock for inclusion in system instructions' intellectual history framework.

---

### Society for US Intellectual History. (2019). How to do intellectual history. Retrieved from https://s-usih.org/2019/04/how-to-do-intellectual-history/

**Reliability:** High - Blog post by professional intellectual historians providing practical methodological guidance from practitioners in the field

**How Used:** Informed the "emblematic thinkers" approach described in research methodology—identifying key figures for each historical period and tracing concepts through their work. Contributed to cross-disciplinary search strategies in knowledge base research methodology section, particularly the recommendation to start with canonical figures then branch out to where concepts appear.

---

### California Learning Resource Network. (2025). What is intellectual history? Retrieved from https://www.clrn.org/what-is-intellectual-history/

**Reliability:** Medium - Educational resource with clear conceptual explanations and contemporary perspectives

**How Used:** Provided framework for understanding intellectual history as tracing feedback loops between abstract thought and concrete reality, considering influence of economic structures, technological paradigms, and cultural contexts. This informed the interdisciplinary concept mapping section's emphasis on examining ideas across multiple domains and understanding how contexts shape conceptual development.

---

### American Association for the Intellectual History of Science (AAIHS). (2016). Five approaches to intellectual history. Retrieved from https://www.aaihs.org/approaches-to-black-intellectual-history/

**Reliability:** High - Professional scholarly organization discussing methodological approaches in the field

**How Used:** Identified five major approaches to intellectual history (social history extension, textual analysis, psychobiography, etc.), which informed the knowledge base's multiple framework options. Reinforced that intellectual history is inherently interdisciplinary and requires methodological pluralism.

---

### Britannica. (1998, updated). Historiography: Intellectual history. Retrieved from https://www.britannica.com/topic/historiography/Intellectual-history

**Reliability:** High - Established encyclopedia with scholarly editorial standards

**How Used:** Provided historical context on evolution of intellectual history as a discipline, including the shift from Lovejoy's unit-ideas approach to more contextual methods. Informed discussion of how concepts' meanings change over time and the importance of avoiding anachronistic interpretations.

---

### Global Intellectual History, Volume 6, Issue 1. (2021). Special issue on interdisciplinarity and methodological pluralism. Taylor & Francis. Retrieved from https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/rgih20/6/1

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed academic journal dedicated to intellectual history scholarship

**How Used:** Reinforced emphasis on methodological pluralism and interdisciplinary approaches in concept tracing. Informed the four concept-mapping frameworks (hub-and-spoke, convergence, parallel development, layered history) by highlighting how intellectual and conceptual history increasingly blur disciplinary boundaries. Contributed to understanding that concepts must be examined across multiple scholarly traditions.

---

### Palti, E. J. (2024). *Intellectual history and the problem of conceptual change*. Cambridge University Press. Retrieved from https://www.cambridge.org/9781009461214

**Reliability:** High - Scholarly monograph from major academic press examining theoretical frameworks for understanding intellectual change

**How Used:** Provided sophisticated frameworks for distinguishing between direct influence, indirect/atmospheric influence, and parallel development in conceptual history. Directly informed case studies section showing different precedent patterns. Contributed to understanding that conceptual change is not a coherent process from a single source but rather converging developments from multiple traditions (Cambridge School, Begriffsgeschichte, French politico-conceptual history).

## SOURCE EVALUATION FRAMEWORKS

### Blakeslee, S., & California State University Chico, Meriam Library. (2010). Evaluating information: Applying the CRAAP test. Retrieved from multiple university library adaptations (2019-2025) including:
- Benedictine University Library. Retrieved from https://researchguides.ben.edu/source-evaluation
- NWACC Library. Retrieved from https://library.nwacc.edu/sourceevaluation/craap
- New Jersey Institute of Technology. Retrieved from https://researchguides.njit.edu/evaluate/CRAAP
- UC Davis Law Library. Retrieved from https://libguides.law.ucdavis.edu/c.php?g=1035998&p=10361056

**Reliability:** High - Academic library resources designed for student instruction in information literacy; CRAAP test developed by professional librarians and widely adopted in higher education

**How Used:** Established CRAAP criteria (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) as foundation for source evaluation framework in both system instructions and knowledge base. These criteria provide systematic approach to assessing source reliability. Multiple university adaptations demonstrated broad consensus on these evaluation principles while also highlighting context-specific applications.

---

### Lowe, M. S., & Macy, K. V. (2021). Questioning CRAAP: A comparison of source evaluation methods. *Journal of the Scholarship of Teaching and Learning*, 21(3), 33-48. Retrieved from https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/EJ1329588.pdf

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed scholarship on information literacy instruction; empirical study comparing evaluation methods

**How Used:** Informed critical perspective on CRAAP test limitations, particularly vulnerability to sophisticated disinformation when used alone. Led to integration of lateral reading strategies (cross-checking information across sources) as essential supplement to CRAAP. Contributed to emphasis in source evaluation framework that vertical reading (examining single source) must be combined with horizontal verification across multiple sources.

---

### EBSCO Research Starters. (n.d.). CRAAP test. Retrieved from https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/social-sciences-and-humanities/craap-test

**Reliability:** High - Educational resource from major academic database provider

**How Used:** Provided comprehensive overview of CRAAP test components and their practical application. Informed detailed breakdown of each criterion (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) in knowledge base Section 3, including specific questions to ask for each component.

---

### Esparrago-Kalidas, A. J. (2021). The effectiveness of CRAAP test in evaluating credibility of sources. *Thesis/Research Study*. Retrieved from https://www.researchgate.net/publication/353978560

**Reliability:** Medium-High - Research study on CRAAP test effectiveness; indicates statistically significant improvement in students' source evaluation abilities

**How Used:** Provided empirical support for CRAAP test's effectiveness as teaching tool for source evaluation, supporting its inclusion as primary framework. Study findings that students struggle to evaluate credibility without systematic framework reinforced need for explicit, structured criteria in GPT instructions.

## CONCEPTUAL PRECEDENTS & PARALLEL DEVELOPMENT

### Palti, E. J. (2024). *Intellectual history and the problem of conceptual change*. Cambridge University Press.

**Reliability:** High - See full entry under Intellectual History Methodology section above

**How Used:** Beyond general methodology, this source specifically informed frameworks for identifying and distinguishing types of conceptual relationships: direct precedents (clear causal influence through citations/documentation), indirect precedents (atmospheric influence or intermediaries), and parallel developments (independent emergence). These distinctions became central to case studies in knowledge base Section 5.

---

### Contributions to the History of Concepts, Volume 16, Issue 2. (2021). Retrieved from https://www.berghahnjournals.com/view/journals/contributions/16/2/

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed journal specializing in conceptual history

**How Used:** Provided examples of how conceptual historians track changes in concepts over time, including methods for identifying when influence was direct versus when ideas emerged in parallel. Informed case study methodology for distinguishing between convergence patterns and parallel developments.

---

### Journal of the Philosophy of History, Volume 14, Issue 2. (2020). Editorial on philosophy of intellectual history and conceptual change. Retrieved from https://brill.com/view/journals/jph/14/2/

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed philosophy journal with focus on historical theory

**How Used:** Contributed to understanding of conceptual stability versus change—how to recognize when a conceptual change has occurred versus when usage remains relatively stable. Informed discussion of temporal aspects of concepts and how meanings accumulate layers over time (Koselleck's "sedimentation" concept).

## CONSPIRACY THEORY HANDLING

### European Commission & UNESCO. (2024). Identifying conspiracy theories. Retrieved from https://commission.europa.eu/strategy-and-policy/coronavirus-response/fighting-disinformation/identifying-conspiracy-theories_en

**Reliability:** High - Official guidance from international organizations; developed with input from Michael Butter (conspiracy theory scholar), John Cook and Stephan Lewandowsky (authors of The Debunking Handbook and The Conspiracy Theory Handbook)

**How Used:** Established six common characteristics of conspiracy theories: alleged secret plot, group of conspirators, 'evidence' that seems supportive, falsely suggesting nothing happens by accident, claiming nothing is as it appears, asserting everything is connected. Informed conspiracy theory identification criteria. Provided best practices for debunking: focus on facts first (not myth), state information is wrong before quoting conspiracy theory, provide fact-based alternative, use visual aids when possible.

---

### Dentith, M. R. X., & Reuter, K. (2023). Normativity in studying conspiracy theory belief: Seven guidelines. *Thinking & Reasoning*. Retrieved from https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09515089.2023.2191644

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed academic article on ethical and methodological standards for conspiracy theory research

**How Used:** Informed ethical guidelines for discussing conspiracy theories without reinforcing them or condescending to believers. Contributed to understanding that conspiracy theories serve psychological needs (knowledge, safety, positive self-regard) even when those needs aren't actually met by the beliefs. Emphasized importance of understanding social and political goods at stake in how we conceptualize and discuss conspiracy theories.

---

### Dyrendal, A., Jolley, D., & Turpin, M. H. (2020). Conspiracy theories in the classroom: Problems and potential solutions. *Religions*, 11(10), 494. Retrieved from https://www.mdpi.com/2077-1444/11/10/494

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed article in academic journal examining educational approaches to conspiracy theories

**How Used:** Provided educational framework that became template for conspiracy theory responses: debunk first (explain why claim lacks scholarly support), then educate (provide evidence-based alternative), then contextualize (explain psychological/social factors without demeaning believers). Emphasized that fact-checking alone is insufficient; must address ethical and political motivations behind conspiracy theories.

---

### Bessi, A. (2021). Moving beyond debunking conspiracy theories from a narrow epistemic lens: Ethical and political implications for education. *Pedagogy, Culture & Society*. Retrieved from https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/14681366.2021.1948911

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed educational research examining pedagogical approaches

**How Used:** Contributed to understanding that narrow fact-checking approaches to conspiracy theories are insufficient and potentially counterproductive. Informed recommendation to address underlying needs and concerns that make conspiracy narratives compelling. Reinforced importance of going beyond epistemic debunking to consider ethical and political dimensions.

---

### Douglas, K. M., et al. (2024). Conspiracy beliefs and consumption: The role of scientific literacy. *Journal of Consumer Research*, 51(4), 656-674. Retrieved from https://academic.oup.com/jcr/article/51/4/656/7643726

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed journal article examining psychological factors in conspiracy belief

**How Used:** Provided evidence that scientific literacy undermines conspiracy beliefs by enabling accurate evaluation of evidence. Informed understanding that conspiracy theories often mimic scientific theories with pseudoscientific evidence, making source evaluation skills critical. Contributed to emphasis on teaching evaluation frameworks rather than simply presenting facts.

---

### Bertuzzi, N. (2021). Conspiracy theories and social movements studies: A research agenda. *Sociology Compass*. Retrieved from https://compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/soc4.12945

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed sociological journal examining methodological and ethical issues in studying conspiracy theories

**How Used:** Highlighted ethical and methodological challenges in researching conspiracy theories, including need for clear operationalization of terms and awareness of how academic discourse about conspiracies can increase distrust. Informed cautions about how to discuss conspiracy theories responsibly without reinforcing them.

---

### Goertzel, T. (2010). Conspiracy theories in science. *EMBO Reports*, 11(7), 493-499. Retrieved from https://www.embopress.org/doi/full/10.1038/embor.2010.84

**Reliability:** High - Peer-reviewed scientific journal; author is sociologist studying conspiracy belief

**How Used:** Provided framework for distinguishing genuine scientific uncertainty from conspiracy theories that target scientific research. Contributed to Template 3 in conspiracy theory responses (contested science vs. conspiracy). Established cascade logic as key marker of conspiracy theories—defenders must implicate more and more people whose failure to discover conspiracy can only be explained by complicity.

## RESEARCH METHODOLOGY NOTES

**Search Strategy:**
Research was conducted in four phases corresponding to the four key areas identified in project planning:
1. Intellectual history methodology and interdisciplinary concept tracing (7 searches)
2. Source reliability evaluation frameworks (6 searches)
3. Methods for identifying conceptual precedents and parallel development (6 searches)
4. Ethical guidelines for discussing conspiracy theories (9 searches)

**Source Selection Criteria:**
- Prioritized peer-reviewed scholarship and academic sources
- Included professional practitioner guidance (library science, intellectual history)
- Balanced theoretical frameworks with practical application examples
- Sought sources representing consensus in respective fields
- Cross-referenced multiple sources to verify methodological approaches

**Limitations:**
- Research conducted primarily through web search; full texts of some academic articles not accessible
- Focus on English-language sources
- Time constraints limited depth of exploration in each area
- Some sources (particularly recent scholarship on conspiracy theories) are from emerging research areas where consensus still developing

## SYNTHESIS & APPLICATION

The sources consulted fall into four complementary categories that together informed the complete GPT system:

1. **Intellectual History Foundations** (Lovejoy, Koselleck, Cambridge School): Provided theoretical frameworks for tracing ideas across time and disciplines

2. **Information Literacy Standards** (CRAAP test, lateral reading): Established systematic criteria for evaluating source reliability

3. **Conceptual Analysis Methods** (Palti, conceptual history journals): Offered frameworks for distinguishing types of influence and precedents

4. **Ethical Communication Guidelines** (UNESCO, conspiracy theory scholarship): Informed responsible handling of contested claims and misinformation

Together, these sources enabled development of:
- Evidence-based research methodologies
- Systematic source evaluation frameworks  
- Sophisticated frameworks for tracing conceptual lineages
- Ethical guardrails for discussing contested topics
- Practical templates and examples demonstrating application
